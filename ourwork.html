Driving Rigorous Research

The J-PAL Africa research team conducts randomized evaluations on social programmes and policies in South Africa

We have hubs in Cape Town and Johannesburg, and work in partnership with local and international researchers, government bodies and NGOs across South Africa.

How our research team works

Through the research process, we:

  • Work with researchers and prospective partners to identify policy-relevant research ideas
  • Help to find workable study designs
  • Support delivery of the intervention and ensure that the integrity of randomisation is maintained
  • Collect data through high-quality survey
  • Support researchers in analysing the data
  • Work with the policy team to synthesise results.

Examples of our research work

Interventions to help young jobseekers signal their skills and find employment

J-PAL Africa’s research team has extensive practice in working with researchers and partners in South Africa to conduct randomized evaluations in the Labour Sector. Some of our partners in South Africa have included South Africa’s Department of Employment and Labour, the City of Cape Town, Harambee, the National Youth Development Agency, Rea Vaya, the National Planning Commission, the City of Johannesburg, and the Africa Microeconomic Research Unit. Researchers have found that programmes focused on reducing job search barriers often improve jobseekers’ employment outcomes.

Key findings:

  • Reference letters increased jobseekers’ chances of getting a response from employers, and encouraging jobseekers to obtain reference letters increased employment rates for women. Researchers: Martin Abel, Rulof Burger, Patrizio Piraino. Read the evaluation summary.
  • Helping young people invest in and plan their job search can improve employment outcomes. In particular, encouraging young people to create a job search action plan increased job offers. Researchers: Martin Abel, Rulof Burger, Eliana Carranza, Patrizio Piraino. Read the evaluation summary.
Forthcoming results:

  • The Impact of Providing Information about Job Seekers’ Skills on Employment in South Africa

Evaluating the impact of prepaid electricity meters

In Cape Town, the research team worked with researchers Kelsey Jack and Grant Smith to conduct a randomized evaluation in Cape Town, South Africa to measure the effect of prepaid meters on households’ electricity use and utility revenue. They found that prepaid meters led households to reduce their electricity consumption, but helped the utility company recover more costs. These findings suggest that prepaid meters can play an important role in maintaining financial viability of utility companies while expanding energy access for the poor. Read the evaluation summary.

The long-term partnership between the researchers, J-PAL Africa, and the City of Cape Town forms the basis for the next frontier in J-PAL Africa’s research work - using administrative data to design and evaluate innovative social programmes.

Building new research to evaluate the roll-out of digital identification and payments innovations

J-PAL Africa’s first research initiative, the Digital Identification and Finance for Africa Initiative (DigiFI) launched in 2019. DigiFI Africa aims to grow the evidence base around innovations in identification and payments by supporting governments in their efforts to monitor and evaluate relevant reforms.

The next frontier: using administrative data to improve evidence-informed decision-making

In a recent Forbes interview, Iqbal Dhaliwal (Executive Director, J-PAL), shared his views on the future of administrative data: “one of the biggest opportunities we have to improve the outcomes of social and development programs is to use large digital data to inform decision making and test innovations.”

In 2019, J-PAL’s Global team launched the Innovations in Data and Experiments for Action Initiative (IDEA) to increase the use of administrative data by governments, non-profits, and private firms for evidence-informed decision-making. In South Africa, J-PAL Africa is creating a long-term partnership with the City of Cape Town that involves a data access portal allowing researchers to use administrative records. In 2020, the administrative data team at J-PAL Africa is looking for new ways to encourage and expand the use of administrative data in research.
